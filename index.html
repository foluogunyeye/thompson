Who Were the Saro?

The Saro — a name derived from "Salone," a corruption of Sierra Leone — were Africans captured during the transatlantic slave trade. Emancipated by the Royal Navy after the 1807 abolition of the slave trade, they were resettled in Freetown, Sierra Leone, where they built communities and gained access to Western education.

Starting in the 1830s, their descendants began migrating back to Nigeria — to Lagos, Abeokuta, and the Niger Delta — where they became known locally as "Saro." Though many were originally Yoruba, the community also included Igbo, Nupe, Hausa, and Efik peoples. What united them was not just origin, but a shared experience of liberation and the determination to rebuild.

A Timeline of Our Story

1807
British Parliament passes the Slave Trade Act. The Royal Navy begins intercepting slave ships and liberating captives.
1820s–1840s
Liberated Africans settle in Freetown. They gain Western education through mission schools and Fourah Bay College, becoming lawyers, doctors, and civil servants.
1830s–1840s
Saro begin migrating back to Nigeria. They settle in Lagos, Abeokuta, and the Niger Delta, establishing communities in Ebute Metta, Olowogbowo, and Yaba.
Mid-1800s
Saro become a dominant commercial force. They pioneer the kola nut trade, establish the oldest churches in Lagos, and expand missionary work across Nigeria.
1840s
Bishop Samuel Ajayi Crowther publishes the first Yoruba primer, grammar book, and dictionary. He translates the Bible into Yoruba, preserving the language for future generations.
1880s
Systemic discrimination by British colonial authorities. Saro are blocked from promotions despite their achievements. They shift from being Anglophiles to becoming cultural nationalists.
Late 1800s
James Johnson leads a revolt among missionaries, demanding greater African involvement. Saro merchants like Richard Beale Blaize pivot into real estate and finance, laying the foundations for modern capital markets in Lagos.
Early 1900s
Herbert Macaulay, a Saro descendant, becomes the father of Nigerian nationalism and leads the struggle for independence.

How We Got Our Surnames

When Africans were liberated from slave ships and brought to Sierra Leone, colonial authorities dismissed their original names as "unpronounceable" or "barbaric." Systematically, liberated Africans were given new English names — often named after the prominent officials, missionaries, and abolitionists in the colony. These surnames became our family names.

Macaulay
Named after Zachary Macaulay
A leading British abolitionist. His cousin Kenneth Macaulay was the colony's first "Superintendent of Captured Negroes."
Johnson
Named after W.A.B. Johnson
A Church Missionary Society missionary who managed the Liberated African village of Regent's Town.
Cole
Named after Thomas Cole
A colonial official in the Liberated African Department responsible for placement of freed captives.
Thompson
Named after Thomas Perronet Thompson
The first Crown Governor of Sierra Leone who oversaw the resettlement of liberated Africans.

Notable Saro People

Herbert Macaulay
Father of Nigerian Nationalism
A Saro descendant who led the anti-colonial movement and fought for Nigerian independence through journalism, political organizing, and intellectual leadership.
Samuel Ajayi Crowther
First African Anglican Bishop
Created the first written Yoruba language system through his primer, grammar book, and dictionary. Translated the Bible into Yoruba, preserving the language.
Funmilayo Ransome-Kuti
Women's Rights Activist
A Saro descendant and women's rights pioneer. Mother of Fela Kuti. Fought against colonial exploitation and gender inequality in Nigeria and across Africa.
Richard Beale Blaize
Pioneer Merchant & Financier
A prominent Saro merchant who pioneered real estate and finance in Lagos, laying the foundations for modern capital markets in Nigeria.
Sarah Forbes Bonetta Davies
Survivor & Royal Goddaughter
Rescued from slavery as a child, she was educated in Sierra Leone and became Queen Victoria's goddaughter, a symbol of possibility and resilience.

Why This Matters to Us

Our ancestors took the tragedy of enslavement and the severe constraints of colonial "freedom" and used them as tools to build the economic, educational, and political frameworks of modern Nigeria. They were not passive victims — they exercised significant agency, petitioning against mistreatment, pioneering industries, preserving Yoruba language and culture, and ultimately laying the groundwork for Nigerian independence.

This is our heritage. We carry their resilience, their intelligence, and their refusal to be defined by the systems that tried to diminish them. Understanding this story is understanding ourselves — who we are, where we come from, and what we're capable of.
